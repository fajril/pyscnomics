Chapter 2: Introduction to Production Sharing Contracts
========================================================

This chapter introduces the fundamental concepts of Production Sharing Contracts
(PSC), the dominant fiscal regime for oil and gas development in Indonesia
and many other producing countries.

2.1 What is a Production Sharing Contract?
-------------------------------------------

A Production Sharing Contract (PSC) is a legal agreement between a government
(representing the state) and a petroleum company (contractor) for the
exploration, development, and production of oil and gas resources.

The PSC framework defines how revenues from hydrocarbon production are shared
between two key parties:

- **The Government** (represented by the national oil company and regulatory
  bodies such as SKK Migas in Indonesia)
- **The Contractor** (the oil company or consortium that operates the field
  and provides capital and expertise)

2.2 Historical Context of Indonesian PSCs
-----------------------------------------

Indonesia introduced the PSC concept in the 1960s as a way to attract foreign
investment in petroleum development while maintaining state ownership of
resources. Over the decades, the PSC framework has evolved:

- **1970s-2016**: Cost Recovery PSC dominated, offering contractors guaranteed
  cost recovery plus profit sharing
- **2017**: Gross Split PSC introduced through PERMEN ESDM No. 8/2017 as the
  mandatory mechanism for new contracts, eliminating cost recovery in favor of
  direct revenue splitting
- **2020**: PERMEN ESDM No. 12/2020 introduced flexibility, allowing the Minister
  to determine contract form based on project risk, investment climate, and state
  benefit. Both Cost Recovery and Gross Split became available options.
- **2024-Present**: PERMEN ESDM No. 13/2024 maintains the dual-scheme approach,
  allowing contractors to choose or even convert between Cost Recovery and
  Gross Split based on project characteristics.

**Current Situation:**

Indonesia now operates a flexible regime where both PSC schemes coexist:

- **Contractor Choice**: Companies can select the scheme that best fits their
  project risk profile and economics
- **Conversion Allowed**: Existing contracts can be converted from one scheme
  to another (subject to approval)
- **Project-Specific**: The Minister determines the appropriate scheme based
  on technical, economic, and strategic factors

This flexibility, welcomed by the Indonesia Petroleum Association (IPA),
allows optimization of fiscal terms for different project types.

2.3 Why PSC Matters in Petroleum Economics
------------------------------------------

The PSC framework is critical for several aspects of petroleum economics:

**Investment Decisions**
   The fiscal terms directly affect project returns and investment attractiveness.
   Understanding the split of revenues is essential for evaluating whether
   a project meets corporate hurdle rates.

**Project Economics Evaluation**
   Accurate cash flow modeling requires understanding how costs are recovered,
   how profits are shared, and how taxes are calculated.

**Contract Negotiations**
   Knowledge of PSC mechanics enables effective negotiation of fiscal terms
   that balance government take with contractor returns.

**Government Take Analysis**
   Analysts must quantify how much of project revenue flows to the government
   versus the contractor to assess fiscal competitiveness.

2.4 The Two Parties of a PSC
----------------------------

**The Government (Host Country)**
The government provides:

- Resource ownership and subsurface rights
- Regulatory framework and licensing
- Infrastructure in some cases
- Fiscal stability through contractual commitments

**The Contractor (Oil Company)**
The contractor provides:

- Capital for exploration and development activities
- Technology and technical expertise
- Operational management and workforce
- Assumption of exploration risk

2.5 Types of PSCs in Indonesia
------------------------------

Indonesia currently operates **two coexisting PSC mechanisms**, with flexibility
for contractors to choose based on project characteristics:

+---------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| Cost Recovery PSC   | Gross Split PSC                           |
+=====================+===========================================+
| Traditional         | Modern mechanism                          |
| mechanism           | introduced in 2017                        |
+---------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| Contractor recovers | No cost recovery -                        |
| costs before profit | direct revenue split                      |
+---------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| Government takes    | More predictable returns                  |
| variable share      | for contractor                            |
+---------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| Complex admin with  | Simplified administration                 |
| depreciation        |                                           |
+---------------------+-------------------------------------------+

**Scheme Selection and Flexibility:**

Under current regulations (PERMEN ESDM No. 13/2024), contractors have several
options:

1. **New Contracts**: The Minister determines the appropriate scheme based on:
   - Project risk level and complexity
   - Investment climate considerations
   - Maximum benefit for the state

2. **Existing Contracts**: Contractors may propose conversion:
   - From Cost Recovery to Gross Split
   - From Gross Split to Cost Recovery
   - Subject to government approval and negotiation

3. **Conversion Benefits**: When converting, operational costs can be
   considered as additional split percentages for the contractor (Pasal 7
   ayat 1 PERMEN ESDM No. 12/2020).

**Selection Considerations:**

Contractors typically choose based on:

- **Cost Recovery PSC**: Preferred for projects with high capital expenditures,
  exploration risk, or uncertain production profiles. The cost recovery
  mechanism provides downside protection.
  
- **Gross Split PSC**: Preferred for projects with lower costs, predictable
  production, and higher margins. The simplified structure offers more
  predictable returns but places more risk on the contractor.

The Indonesia Petroleum Association (IPA) supports this flexibility, noting
that "each oil and gas project has different characteristics. The choice of
contract scheme depends on the existing project characteristics."

This textbook covers both mechanisms in detail. Chapter 6 examines the
Cost Recovery mechanism, while Chapter 7 covers the Gross Split mechanism.

2.6 Key PSC Terminology
------------------------

Before proceeding, familiarize yourself with these essential terms:

.. glossary::
   :sorted:

   First Tranche Petroleum (FTP)
      A portion of total revenue allocated before cost recovery calculations,
      providing the government with priority revenue regardless of costs.

   Cost Recovery
      The mechanism where contractors recover eligible costs from gross revenue
      before profit splitting occurs.

   Unrecovered Cost
      Costs in a period that exceed the cost recovery limit, carried forward
      to subsequent periods.

   Equity To Be Shared (ETS)
      The remaining revenue after deducting FTP and cost recovery, available
      for splitting between contractor and government.

   Domestic Market Obligation (DMO)
      Requirement to sell a portion of production to the domestic market at
      a concessionary price.

   Profit Petroleum
      The portion of production (or revenue) remaining after cost recovery,
      which is shared between the parties.

   Investment Credit (IC)
      An additional benefit where a percentage of capital costs can be credited
      directly against the government's take, effectively accelerating cost
      recovery.

   Depreciation
      The systematic allocation of capital costs over the useful life of the
      asset. In PSC economics, depreciation is used to reduce taxable income.

   Amortization
      Similar to depreciation but for intangible assets (such as geological
      surveys, drilling costs, permits). Typically calculated using the Unit
      of Production method in Gross Split contracts.

   Cash Flow
      The net cash movement in a given period, combining all revenue, costs,
      and tax payments.

   Government Take
      The total share of revenue received by the government including taxes,
      profit splits, and other fiscal obligations.

   Contractor Take
      The total share of revenue received by the contractor after all costs,
      taxes, and government obligations have been paid.

   Capital Expenditure (CAPEX)
      Investments in long-term assets such as drilling rigs, production facilities,
      pipelines, and platforms. These are depreciated over their useful life.

   Operating Expenditure (OPEX)
      Ongoing costs to operate the field, including fixed costs (supervisory staff)
      and variable costs (chemicals, fuel, maintenance).

   Net Present Value (NPV)
      The present value of all future cash flows, discounted at the project's
      hurdle rate. Positive NPV indicates value creation.

   Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
      The discount rate at which NPV equals zero. The IRR is compared against
      the hurdle rate to assess project viability.

   Payout Time (POT)
      The time when cumulative cash flows turn positive, indicating the point
      at which the project has recovered all invested capital.
